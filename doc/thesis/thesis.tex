\begin{center}
  \textbf{ABSTRACT}
\end{center}

\vspace{1cm}

The rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs) has created new
opportunities for automating educational content creation. However,
generating high-quality multiple-choice questions (MCQs) for academic use
remains a non-trivial challenge: manual question creation is
time-consuming, generic LLM generation is prone to hallucination, and
off-the-shelf outputs rarely conform to an institution's specific exam
style, difficulty distribution, or cognitive-level requirements.

In this thesis, I propose \textbf{QuizGen}, a pattern-aware
retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) platform for generating grounded
multiple-choice questions from lecture materials. QuizGen addresses the
limitations of generic generation by combining lecture-material retrieval,
exam-pattern conditioning, and Bloom's taxonomy control within a unified
web-based workflow.

The system operates through a multi-stage pipeline. Uploaded lecture
documents in PDF, DOCX, and PPTX formats are extracted and split into
overlapping chunks with a chunk size of 2{,}000 and an overlap of 200.
These chunks are embedded using
\textit{gemini\mbox{-}embedding\mbox{-}001} and stored for semantic
retrieval. When a lecturer requests a quiz, the most relevant chunks are
retrieved through cosine similarity and injected into a structured prompt
together with an optional exam-pattern template extracted from past exams.
The prompt also encodes a target Bloom's taxonomy level and difficulty
distribution so that the LLM produces questions with answer options, the
correct answer, an explanation, a Bloom level label, and a difficulty
rating. Each generated question is then scored by an automated grounding
evaluator that measures semantic overlap with the source chunks, flagging
questions as \textit{grounded}, \textit{partially grounded}, or
\textit{weak}. Finally, the questions are published as a playable quiz;
student attempts, scores, timings, and Bloom-level accuracy are stored and
visualized in a per-quiz confidence-trend dashboard.

Evaluation on ten topics in both English and Vietnamese, with three
repeated runs and six questions per topic, shows that adding RAG improves
semantic grounding over vanilla LLM generation. Incorporating exam-pattern
conditioning further improves Bloom-level distribution alignment. The full
system also achieves the highest LLM judge quality score among the tested
baselines while maintaining complete question-set generation in every run.

Nonetheless, several limitations remain. The evaluation dataset is
thesis-scale and may not generalize to all domains or languages. The
grounding score is a semantic proxy and does not guarantee factual
correctness. Lecturer review before formal exam use is therefore still
recommended.

\bigskip
\noindent\textbf{Keywords:} multiple-choice question generation,
retrieval-augmented generation, large language models, Bloom's taxonomy,
exam-pattern conditioning, educational technology.

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{Background}

The rapid development of artificial intelligence has created new
opportunities for educational technology, especially in tasks that involve
content understanding, summarization, tutoring support, and question
generation. Large language models (LLMs), in particular, can generate
fluent natural-language outputs and can assist educators in preparing
learning materials more efficiently. In the context of assessment, this
capability is useful because many courses require frequent quizzes,
practice exercises, and exam-style questions.

Multiple-choice questions (MCQs) are among the most commonly used
assessment formats. They are structured, easy to grade automatically, and
suitable for both formative assessment and summative examination. A
well-designed MCQ can test not only factual recall but also conceptual
understanding and higher-order reasoning. For students, MCQs also provide a
practical way to review course knowledge and identify weaknesses before an
exam.

However, producing good MCQs is not a simple task. Lecturers must ensure
that each question is relevant to the lecture materials, has one clearly
correct answer, includes plausible distractors, and matches the expected
difficulty level. When a course contains many lecture slides or reading
materials, manually creating a large number of questions becomes
time-consuming. Furthermore, question quality may vary depending on the
lecturer's available time, experience, and familiarity with the intended
assessment pattern.

Although LLMs can generate questions quickly, direct generation without
source control may produce questions that are fluent but unsupported by the
actual teaching materials. This problem is important in an academic setting,
where generated questions should be traceable to the content that students
have learned. Therefore, question generation should not only be automatic,
but also grounded in lecture materials and aligned with the desired exam
style.

These observations motivate the development of QuizGen, a pattern-aware
retrieval-augmented generation system for creating multiple-choice questions
from lecture materials. The system is designed to support lecturers in
generating reviewable questions and to support students in practicing those
questions through a web-based learning workflow.

\subsection{Problem Statement}

Current MCQ creation still depends heavily on manual effort. In a typical
course, lecturers need to review lecture materials, identify important
concepts, decide the intended difficulty level, write the question stem,
prepare answer options, and verify that the final question is correct. This
process is repetitive and can become burdensome when many quizzes are needed
across different topics.

Recent LLMs offer a promising solution because they can produce
natural-language questions with little input. Nevertheless, using an LLM
directly for academic question generation introduces several problems.

First, generated questions may not be grounded in the uploaded lecture
materials. A model may rely on its general knowledge and generate content
that appears reasonable but is not actually covered in the course. In
educational assessment, this is a serious issue because students should be
tested on the intended learning materials, not on unsupported external
content.

Second, generic LLM outputs may not follow a specific exam pattern. In
practice, each course may have its own style of questions, answer-option
structure, and difficulty distribution. Without additional conditioning,
generated questions may be inconsistent with the style that lecturers
expect.

Third, it is difficult to control the cognitive level of generated
questions. Educational design often uses Bloom's taxonomy to distinguish
between different levels of learning, such as remembering, understanding,
applying, and analyzing. If this distribution is not controlled, the
generated quiz may contain too many simple recall questions or may fail to
match the intended learning objectives.

Fourth, many question generation approaches stop at producing question text.
They do not provide a complete workflow for students to attempt the generated
quiz, review their answers, track scores, or observe learning progress over
time. As a result, the generated questions are less useful as part of a
practical learning system.

Therefore, the main problem addressed in this thesis is how to design and
implement a system that can generate exam-style MCQs that are grounded in
uploaded lecture materials, aligned with a target question pattern, and
usable within an interactive quiz practice workflow.

\subsection{Scope and Objectives}

The main objective of this thesis is to design, implement, and evaluate
QuizGen, a web-based system for generating grounded and pattern-aware
multiple-choice questions from lecture materials.

The system focuses on a complete workflow in which lecturers can upload
lecture materials, configure or select a question pattern, generate MCQs,
review the generated results, and publish them as a playable quiz. Students
can then attempt the quiz, view their results, and track their performance
through the application.

The specific objectives of this thesis are as follows:

\begin{itemize}
  \item To build a document processing pipeline that supports lecture
        materials in PDF, DOCX, and PPTX formats.
  \item To apply retrieval-augmented generation so that generated questions
        are based on relevant content chunks retrieved from the uploaded
        materials.
  \item To incorporate exam-pattern conditioning so that generated questions
        can better follow a desired question style and structure.
  \item To generate structured MCQ outputs, including question text, answer
        options, correct answer, explanation, difficulty level, and Bloom's
        taxonomy label.
  \item To provide a grounding evaluation mechanism that helps identify
        whether a generated question is strongly or weakly supported by the
        source materials.
  \item To implement a web application that supports quiz generation,
        review, practice, attempt history, and performance visualization.
  \item To evaluate the system using thesis-scale experiments and compare
        the full system with baseline generation approaches.
\end{itemize}

Within the scope of this thesis, QuizGen is limited to multiple-choice
question generation. The system is designed as a local thesis-scale
prototype rather than a high-concurrency production platform. The current
implementation uses a SQLite database and integrates LLM-based services for
embedding, retrieval, and generation. The evaluation is conducted on a
limited dataset covering English and Vietnamese topics.

Several aspects are outside the current scope. The system does not replace
lecturer review for formal examinations. It does not guarantee that every
generated question is fully correct, even when a grounding score is
provided. The grounding score is treated as a support signal rather than a
perfect factuality detector. Large-scale classroom deployment, production
database migration, and human expert evaluation are left for future work.

\subsection{Assumptions and Proposed Solution}

This thesis assumes that the uploaded lecture materials are relevant,
readable, and sufficiently informative for question generation. It also
assumes that lecturers or authorized users will review generated questions
before using them in formal assessment. The intended application context is
a university or classroom environment where lecturers need to create
practice or exam-style questions and students need additional opportunities
for self-practice.

To address the identified problems, this thesis proposes QuizGen, a
pattern-aware retrieval-augmented generation system. The system combines
semantic retrieval, exam-pattern conditioning, Bloom-level control,
grounding evaluation, and quiz practice features in a unified web
application.

The proposed workflow begins with document upload and text extraction. The
extracted lecture content is divided into overlapping chunks and converted
into vector embeddings. When a quiz is generated, the system retrieves the
most relevant chunks using semantic similarity and inserts them into the
generation prompt. The prompt may also include an exam pattern derived from
sample questions, together with the desired difficulty and Bloom-level
distribution.

After generation, each MCQ contains a question stem, answer options, a
correct answer, an explanation, a difficulty label, and a Bloom's taxonomy
label. The system then evaluates the source support of each question using a
grounding mechanism. Generated questions can be reviewed and used as a
playable quiz. Student attempts, scores, timing, and performance trends are
stored and displayed through the dashboard.

Through this design, QuizGen aims to reduce the manual effort required for
MCQ preparation while keeping the generated questions connected to lecture
materials and usable in a practical learning workflow.

\section{Literature Review}

\subsection{Large Language Models in Education}

Large language models (LLMs) are neural networks trained on massive text
corpora that can produce fluent, contextually appropriate natural language
across a wide range of tasks \cite{brown2020gpt3}. In education, LLMs have
attracted growing interest as tools for content summarization, intelligent
tutoring, automated grading, and question generation. Xu et al.\
\cite{xu2024survey1} provide a comprehensive survey of LLM applications in
education, organising the landscape around four pillars: student assistance,
teacher assistance, adaptive learning, and educational assessment. The survey
concludes that LLMs ``perform outstandingly in improving teaching efficiency
and interactivity'' but also face substantial challenges in data privacy,
model transparency, and domain-specific factual accuracy.

A parallel survey by Xu et al.\ \cite{xu2024survey2} extends this analysis
by cataloguing benchmarks and evaluation datasets specific to educational AI.
The authors observe that most existing systems focus on general-purpose
question answering and reading comprehension rather than structured assessment
generation tied to lecture materials. This gap motivates research that places
LLM generation within a curriculum-grounded pipeline.

Empirical studies confirm both the promise and the limitations of LLMs for
classroom use. A systematic review published in \textit{Frontiers in
Education} \cite{frontiers2024chatgpt} examined 47 studies and found that
ChatGPT-based tools improve student engagement and provide useful formative
feedback, but show uneven performance across subjects and tend to reduce
critical-thinking demands when used without appropriate scaffolding. A case
study by Pham et al.\ \cite{ieeebigdata2023} at IEEE BigData 2023 evaluated
ChatGPT (GPT-3.5-turbo) for question generation from passage context and
demonstrated that prompt engineering alone, without retrieval grounding, can
produce competitive outputs in well-scoped domains.

Hallucination remains the most critical risk for educational deployment.
Danyaro et al.\ \cite{danyaro2024hallucination} identify hallucination as a
fundamental reliability barrier, noting that LLMs may produce plausible but
factually incorrect statements, particularly in specialised academic domains.
The authors recommend knowledge injection, human review loops, and
source-grounded generation as primary mitigation strategies. These findings
directly motivate the retrieval-augmented approach adopted in this thesis.

\subsection{Retrieval-Augmented Generation}

Retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) \cite{lewis2020rag} is a framework
that augments a generative language model with a retrieval component. Given
an input query, the retrieval module selects relevant passages from an
external knowledge store; these passages are then concatenated with the
query and passed to the generator as additional context. Lewis et al.\
\cite{lewis2020rag} demonstrate that this architecture outperforms
closed-book generation on knowledge-intensive NLP tasks such as open-domain
question answering and fact verification, because the retrieved context
constrains the generator to produce responses supported by authoritative
source material.

In the context of educational question generation, RAG addresses the core
weakness of vanilla LLM prompting: the tendency to hallucinate content not
covered in the course. By indexing lecture materials and retrieving the most
relevant chunks at generation time, the system ensures that each MCQ is
anchored to a specific segment of the uploaded document. This traceability
is particularly valuable in academic settings where assessment questions must
be justifiable from the course syllabus.

Evaluating RAG systems introduces its own methodological challenges. Es et
al.\ \cite{es2023ragas} propose RAGAS, a reference-free evaluation framework
that measures retrieval quality (context recall and precision) and generation
quality (faithfulness and answer relevance) without requiring gold-standard
annotations. RAGAS treats faithfulness, defined as the degree to which
generated statements are entailed by the retrieved context, as the primary
grounding signal. This thesis adopts a semantic grounding score inspired by
this principle, adapted for the MCQ generation domain where ground-truth
answer keys are available but annotated question quality is not.

\subsection{Prompt Engineering and Few-Shot Learning}

Prompt engineering refers to the systematic design of input instructions to
elicit desired behaviours from a pre-trained language model without updating
its parameters \cite{weng2023prompteng}. It offers a lightweight alternative
to fine-tuning, requiring no labelled training data and no GPU-intensive
optimisation, making it practical for domain-specific applications where
training data is scarce or expensive to collect.

White et al.\ \cite{white2023prompt} introduce a prompt pattern catalog that
formalises common prompting strategies as reusable templates. Relevant
patterns for this thesis include the \textit{Persona pattern} (assigning the
model a role such as ``You are a university lecturer in Computer Science''),
the \textit{Template pattern} (providing output schemas such as JSON
structures for MCQ fields), and the \textit{Few-shot example pattern}
(including representative questions in the prompt to demonstrate the target
style). Together, these patterns form the basis of exam-pattern conditioning
in QuizGen.

Few-shot learning \cite{brown2020gpt3} allows a model to generalise from a
small number of in-context examples. In the QuizGen pipeline, sample
questions extracted from past exams serve as few-shot demonstrations of the
desired question style, difficulty level, and answer-option structure. This
conditioning mechanism does not require access to model weights and is
therefore compatible with API-based LLM providers. A detailed description of
the prompt structure and conditioning logic is provided in Chapter III.

\subsection{Bloom's Taxonomy and Difficulty Control}

Bloom's taxonomy \cite{bloom1956taxonomy} organises cognitive learning
objectives into six hierarchical levels: \textit{Remember},
\textit{Understand}, \textit{Apply}, \textit{Analyze}, \textit{Evaluate},
and \textit{Create}. Originally published in 1956 and revised in 2001, the
framework remains a standard tool in educational design for specifying and
assessing the cognitive demands of learning activities.

In the context of automated question generation, Bloom's taxonomy serves two
distinct functions. First, it provides a vocabulary for \textit{controlling}
the cognitive level of generated questions: by encoding a target Bloom label
in the generation prompt, the system can steer the LLM toward questions that
require recall, application, or analysis. Second, it provides a metric for
\textit{evaluating} the cognitive balance of a generated question set: the
Kullback--Leibler (KL) divergence between the target Bloom distribution and
the observed distribution of generated labels measures how closely the system
honours the intended difficulty mix.

Research in neural question generation confirms the practical relevance of
this control mechanism. A survey of neural question generation systems
presented at IJCAI 2024 \cite{ijcai2024nqg} identifies cognitive-level
targeting as an open challenge, noting that most existing systems generate
lower-order recall questions by default and require explicit conditioning to
produce higher-order analytical questions. QuizGen addresses this limitation
by including the target Bloom level and difficulty distribution as explicit
prompt parameters, and by measuring KL divergence as a primary evaluation
metric.

\subsection{Existing Question Generation Systems and Tools}

A number of systems have been proposed or deployed for automatic question
generation. A systematic literature review of transformer- and LLM-based MCQ
generation methods published at IEEE in 2025 \cite{ieee2025mcqsurvey}
identifies fine-tuning and prompt engineering as the two dominant paradigms,
and notes that most systems optimise for generation fluency but do not
evaluate grounding or cognitive-level alignment.

The \textbf{E-QGen} system \cite{eqgen2024} generates questions from lecture
abstracts using GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 as backends. E-QGen demonstrates that
structuring prompts with role, task, context, and format components improves
output relevance, but the system does not retrieve source evidence and
provides no grounding evaluation. The \textbf{BUE AQG System}
\cite{bueaqg2024} combines BERT-based keyword extraction, T5-based question
generation, and GPT-based answer generation, achieving an 80\% lecturer
satisfaction rate in user studies. However, it does not support exam-pattern
conditioning or quiz practice workflows. A study presented at COLING 2025
\cite{coling2025mcq} applied prompt engineering to university regulation
documents and demonstrated effective MCQ generation, but similarly lacked
integration with a practice interface or performance analytics.

Commercial tools such as the \textbf{GPT Quiz Generator for Google Forms}
provide practical MCQ generation across multiple languages using GPT-4o
mini, but offer no retrieval grounding, no Bloom-level control, and no
student attempt tracking. \textbf{Generic ChatGPT prompting}, used as the
vanilla baseline in this thesis, represents the simplest deployment scenario:
direct LLM generation without any source retrieval or pattern conditioning.

\subsection{Research Gap}

The preceding review reveals a consistent pattern across existing systems:
research and commercial tools tend to address question generation in
isolation, optimising for fluency or accuracy while neglecting the broader
assessment workflow. Specifically, four gaps remain unaddressed in the
existing literature.

First, most systems generate questions without retrieval grounding. They
rely on the LLM's parametric knowledge rather than the specific lecture
materials uploaded by a lecturer, making the output unverifiable and
potentially irrelevant to the course content.

Second, exam-pattern conditioning is largely absent. No reviewed system
allows a lecturer to supply sample exam questions and use them to condition
the style, structure, and difficulty distribution of the generated output.

Third, Bloom's taxonomy control is not integrated into the generation
pipeline. While educational theory recommends distributing assessment
questions across cognitive levels, existing systems do not enforce or
evaluate this distribution.

Fourth, no reviewed system integrates question generation with a student
practice workflow. The generated questions are treated as a one-time output
rather than as content for an interactive learning environment with attempt
tracking, score history, and confidence trend visualisation.

QuizGen addresses all four gaps within a single unified system. It combines
semantic retrieval from uploaded lecture materials, exam-pattern conditioning
via few-shot prompt injection, explicit Bloom-level and difficulty control,
a grounding evaluation component, and a web-based quiz practice interface
with per-quiz performance analytics. This combination distinguishes QuizGen
from all systems reviewed in this chapter and positions it as a more
complete solution for lecture-grounded, pattern-aware MCQ generation and
practice.

\section{Methodology and System Design}

\subsection{Proposed Framework Overview}

QuizGen is designed as a nine-stage pipeline that transforms raw lecture
materials into playable, grounded multiple-choice quizzes with performance
analytics. Figure~\ref{fig:framework} illustrates the complete workflow.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \fbox{%
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\linewidth}
      \centering
      \vspace{0.3cm}
      \textbf{Stage 1:} User uploads PDF / DOCX / PPTX\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 2:} Text extraction and overlapping chunking\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 3:} Chunk embedding via \textit{gemini-embedding-001}\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 4:} Semantic retrieval of top-$k$ relevant chunks\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 5:} Optional exam-pattern injection into prompt\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 6:} MCQ generation via LLM (Groq / Gemini fallback)\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 7:} Grounding evaluation per generated question\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 8:} Quiz practice — attempt submission and scoring\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      \textbf{Stage 9:} Dashboard — confidence trend and Bloom analytics\\[0.3cm]
    \end{minipage}%
  }
  \caption{Overall QuizGen pipeline from document upload to analytics.}
  \label{fig:framework}
\end{figure}

The system is implemented as a two-tier web application. The backend is
built with FastAPI (Python) and exposes a RESTful API secured with JWT
authentication. The frontend is built with Next.js and React, providing a
guided four-step wizard that walks a lecturer from document selection
through pattern configuration, generation, and review. Data is persisted in
a SQLite database, making the system self-contained and suitable for
thesis-scale local deployment.

The LLM layer uses a configurable fallback chain. The primary provider is
Groq serving the \textit{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} model; if the primary
provider is rate-limited, the system automatically retries with alternative
Groq models and then with Gemini as a final fallback. Embeddings are always
computed using the \textit{gemini-embedding-001} model via the Google
Generative Language API and are not subject to the generation fallback
chain.

\subsection{Document Processing and Chunking}

When a user uploads a lecture file, the backend extracts its raw text
content and splits it into overlapping character-level chunks. Three file
formats are supported: PDF, DOCX, and PPTX. Each format is handled by a
dedicated parser in the document processor service, which normalises the
extracted text before chunking.

Chunking is necessary because LLM context windows impose token limits, and
embedding quality degrades when inputs are too long. The system applies a
fixed-size chunking strategy with a configurable overlap. Table~\ref{tab:chunking}
summarises the document processing configuration used in this thesis.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \caption{Document processing configuration.}
  \label{tab:chunking}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Parameter} & \textbf{Value} \\
    \midrule
    Supported formats    & PDF, DOCX, PPTX \\
    Chunk size           & 2{,}000 characters \\
    Chunk overlap        & 200 characters \\
    Embedding model      & \textit{gemini-embedding-001} \\
    Retrieval top-$k$    & 3 chunks (evaluation setting) \\
    Maximum upload size  & 50 MB \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The overlap of 200 characters ensures that concepts spanning a chunk
boundary are not severed. When a sentence begins near the end of one chunk
and continues into the next, both chunks independently retain enough
context for the embedding model to capture the full semantic meaning. This
is particularly important for definitions and multi-step explanations that
are common in lecture slides.

\subsection{Embedding and Retrieval}

After chunking, each text segment is converted into a dense vector
representation using the \textit{gemini-embedding-001} embedding model.
These vectors are stored in the \texttt{chunk\_embeddings} table alongside
the chunk text and a reference to the parent document. Because embeddings
are precomputed at upload time, retrieval at generation time requires no
additional embedding API calls for the source side.

When a lecturer initiates a generation request, the system constructs a
query from the generation parameters (topic, language, and question count)
and embeds it using the same model. The cosine similarity between the query
embedding and each stored chunk embedding is computed, and the top-$k$
chunks with the highest similarity scores are selected as the source
context. This process is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:retrieval}.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \fbox{%
    \begin{minipage}{0.82\linewidth}
      \centering
      \vspace{0.3cm}
      User submits generation request\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      Backend embeds query $\rightarrow$ query vector $\mathbf{q}$\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      Compute $\cos(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{c}_i)$ for each stored chunk $\mathbf{c}_i$\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      Select top-$k$ chunks by cosine similarity\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      Concatenate chunk texts as prompt context\\[4pt]
      $\downarrow$\\[4pt]
      Pass context + instructions to LLM\\[0.3cm]
    \end{minipage}%
  }
  \caption{RAG retrieval sequence from query to prompt context.}
  \label{fig:retrieval}
\end{figure}

The selected chunks are joined with separator tokens and inserted into the
generation prompt as the \textit{Source Material} section. This ensures
that every generated question has access to relevant lecture content at
generation time, directly reducing the risk of hallucinated or unsupported
questions.

\subsection{Exam Pattern Extraction}

QuizGen allows a lecturer to create a named \textit{exam pattern} by
providing sample exam questions and configuration parameters. The pattern
is stored as a JSON document in the \texttt{patterns} table and captures
three types of information: the question type distribution (for example,
70\% MCQ and 30\% short answer), the average question length in words, and
a set of up to five sample questions that serve as few-shot demonstrations
of the desired exam style.

When a pattern is selected during generation, the system injects its
configuration and sample questions directly into the generation prompt.
The sample questions are presented under the heading \textit{Example
Questions (match this style and format)}, instructing the LLM to reproduce
the same phrasing patterns, option structure, and difficulty feel as the
provided examples. This conditioning mechanism requires no model fine-tuning
and is therefore compatible with any API-based LLM provider.

Figure~\ref{fig:pattern-step} shows the pattern configuration step in the
QuizGen web interface.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.92\linewidth]{02-workflow-pattern.png}
  \caption{Exam pattern and difficulty configuration step in the QuizGen wizard.}
  \label{fig:pattern-step}
\end{figure}

\subsection{MCQ Generation Method}

The generation prompt is assembled from four components: (1) the retrieved
source chunks as the \textit{Source Material}, (2) generation instructions
specifying the number of questions, question types, and target language,
(3) an optional difficulty distribution specifying the percentage of easy,
medium, and hard questions, and (4) the optional exam-pattern block
containing sample questions and style metadata.

The assembled prompt is sent to the primary LLM provider, which is Groq
serving the \textit{llama-3.3-70b-versatile} model. The system instructs
the model to return a valid JSON array and does not allow markdown code
fences or free-text preambles in the response. Each element of the array
corresponds to one generated question and must conform to the schema
described in Table~\ref{tab:mcq-schema}.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \caption{MCQ output schema.}
  \label{tab:mcq-schema}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Field} & \textbf{Type} & \textbf{Description} \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{type}        & string  & Question type: \texttt{mcq}, \texttt{true\_false}, \texttt{fill\_blank} \\
    \texttt{question}    & string  & The question stem \\
    \texttt{options}     & array   & Four labelled options (A–D) for MCQ; empty otherwise \\
    \texttt{answer}      & string  & The correct answer \\
    \texttt{explanation} & string  & Brief explanation referencing the source material \\
    \texttt{difficulty}  & string  & \texttt{easy}, \texttt{medium}, or \texttt{hard} \\
    \texttt{bloom\_level} & string & One of six Bloom levels (see Section~\ref{subsec:bloom}) \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The Bloom level field is populated by the LLM based on the cognitive demand
of the question. The six valid values are \texttt{remember},
\texttt{understand}, \texttt{apply}, \texttt{analyze}, \texttt{evaluate},
and \texttt{create}, corresponding to the revised Bloom's taxonomy
\cite{bloom1956taxonomy}. When a difficulty distribution is specified in
the prompt, the model is instructed to generate the target number of
questions at each difficulty level as closely as possible.

\subsection{Grounding and Hallucination Proxy}
\label{subsec:grounding}

After generation, each MCQ is scored by the grounding evaluator, which
estimates the degree to which the question text, correct answer, and
explanation are supported by the source chunks. The evaluator concatenates
these three fields into a single passage and computes its cosine similarity
against the source document embedding. Because raw cosine similarity lies
in $[-1, 1]$, it is normalised to $[0, 1]$ using the transformation
$s = (\text{cos} + 1) / 2$ before applying the grounding thresholds.

Table~\ref{tab:grounding} lists the three grounding status levels and their
corresponding score ranges.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \caption{Grounding status levels and semantic score thresholds.}
  \label{tab:grounding}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}lll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Status} & \textbf{Score range} & \textbf{Interpretation} \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{well\_grounded}      & $s > 0.70$          & Strong semantic overlap with source \\
    \texttt{partially\_grounded} & $0.45 < s \leq 0.70$ & Moderate overlap; review recommended \\
    \texttt{poorly\_grounded}    & $s \leq 0.45$        & Weak overlap; likely unsupported \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

In addition to the semantic score, the evaluator computes a keyword overlap
baseline by comparing the set of content tokens (words of three or more
characters) in the generated passage against the source document tokens.
This secondary metric provides an interpretable count of matched terms and
helps the lecturer identify which specific source words were covered. The
grounding score is reported as a review aid; it highlights questions that
may require additional validation but does not guarantee factual correctness
for well-grounded questions.

\subsection{Quiz Practice and Confidence Trend}

Once a generation is reviewed and saved, it becomes a named quiz that
any authenticated user can attempt. The quiz practice interface presents
one question at a time, records the user's selected option, and tracks
elapsed time. On submission, the backend stores the attempt in the
\texttt{quiz\_attempts} table, recording the generation identifier, the
user's answers, the final score, the total time taken, and per-question
Bloom-level correctness.

The confidence trend is computed per quiz as the sequence of attempt score
percentages ordered by submission time. For a given quiz, if a user
attempts it multiple times, the trend shows whether their score is
improving, stable, or declining. This longitudinal view is displayed in the
dashboard as a line chart per named generation.

workflow.

\subsection{Database Design}
\label{subsec:bloom}

The system uses a single SQLite database with seven core tables.
Table~\ref{tab:entities} lists each entity and its purpose.

\begin{table}[H]
  \centering
  \singlespacing
  \caption{Database entities and their roles.}
  \label{tab:entities}
  \begin{tabular}{@{}ll@{}}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Entity} & \textbf{Role} \\
    \midrule
    \texttt{users}            & Account identity, hashed password, and role \\
    \texttt{documents}        & Uploaded files, raw text, and processed chunks \\
    \texttt{chunk\_embeddings} & Chunk vectors for semantic retrieval \\
    \texttt{patterns}         & Exam templates, sample questions, style config \\
    \texttt{generations}      & Generated quiz records with prompt and config snapshot \\
    \texttt{quiz\_attempts}   & User answers, scores, timing, and Bloom accuracy \\
    \texttt{api\_calls}       & Provider usage, token count, and latency logs \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The key relationships are as follows. A \texttt{user} owns many
\texttt{documents} and \texttt{patterns}. A \texttt{generation} references
one \texttt{document} (as source) and optionally one \texttt{pattern} (as
style guide). A \texttt{quiz\_attempt} references one \texttt{generation}
and one \texttt{user}. The \texttt{api\_calls} table records every outbound
LLM and embedding call for usage tracking and provider-level diagnostics.

field that captures the exact chunk size, embedding model, prompt version,
and provider used for each run. This reproducibility artifact ensures that
evaluation results reported in Chapter~V can be traced back to a specific
system configuration.
